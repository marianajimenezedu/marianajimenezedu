Raíces: Rooted in Culture, Rising in Language
Ph.D. Doctoral Research · Texas Tech University · SLAR Grade 4 Biliteracy Curriculum · 2026–Present
Project Overview

Raíces is an original, identity-centered biliteracy curriculum for Grade 4 dual language classrooms in South Texas — developed as doctoral research in Curriculum & Instruction: Language, Diversity & Literacy Studies at Texas Tech University. It is designed to center the linguistic and cultural identities of Mexican American and multilingual learners while building rigorous, TEKS-aligned Spanish language arts skills. Every unit integrates explicit language objectives, translanguaging supports, and formative assessment — grounding practitioner design in scholarly inquiry.

Lesson Architecture — 5-Step Micro-Framework
Conectar
10 min
Activate prior knowledge and cultural connection. Students connect the day's theme to lived experience through a culturally grounded provocation — honoring home language, family stories, and community knowledge as academic assets.
Caja de Herramientas
10 min
Language tools and metalinguistic focus. Explicit language objective introduced with function label (Narrar · Clasificar · Sintetizar). Sentence plantillas modeled by teacher. Vocabulary is discovered in context through El Partido — not pre-taught.
Explorar
20 min
Deep engagement with anchor text and content. Structured reading, listening (TEKS 4.1A audio integration), and discussion tasks. Tiered questioning scaffolds access for emergent bilinguals and SpEd students without reducing rigor.
Crear
15 min
Language production with purpose. Students use plantillas and lesson vocabulary to produce original written or oral text. Translanguaging is strategically planned — not permissive code-switching — with explicit ACTFL proficiency goals.
Compartir
5 min
Community knowledge-building and formative assessment. Students share with partners or class; teacher conducts an exit observation protocol. Data informs next-day differentiation without adding grading load.

Biliteracy Bridge Library
Live Digital Tool · Ed Tech Para Todos · biliteracybridge.softr.app · 2025–Present
Project Overview

The Biliteracy Bridge Library is a free, educator-facing digital platform that enables K–8 bilingual and dual language teachers to discover, filter, and deploy bilingual and bicultural texts — with full instructional scaffolding built into every entry. It was designed in direct response to a gap identified through years of classroom practice: educators who wanted to use culturally sustaining texts could not efficiently find resources that were level-appropriate, thematically aligned, and instructionally ready without hours of individual research.

What Each Entry Includes
  • Language Objectives — ACTFL-aligned productive and receptive objectives mapped to each text
  • Biliteracy Skill Focus — cross-linguistic transfer opportunities explicitly identified
  • Teacher Lens Notes — cultural specificity guidance and potential classroom sensitivities flagged
  • Translanguaging Supports — strategically planned language bridging activities
  • Writing Extensions — cross-linguistic composition prompts tied to each text's themes
  • Filter System — searchable by theme, genre, language, age group, and biliteracy level
  • Heritage Language Tag — texts that center heritage language maintenance are flagged separately
  • Cultural Community Tag — entries organized by represented community for intentional selection

Translanguaging with Purpose
Conference Presentation · SAAABE · San Antonio, TX · 2026
Presentation Overview
Translanguaging as Instructional Strategy: Bridging Research and Practice for Multilingual Learners
San Antonio Association for Bilingual Education (SAAABE) · San Antonio, Texas · 2026

This practitioner-research presentation challenged the field's tendency to treat translanguaging as permissive code-switching — and reframed it as a strategically planned instructional move explicitly tied to ACTFL proficiency goals. Drawing on classroom evidence from dual language practice and doctoral research, the presentation modeled how translanguaging can be embedded into lesson architecture without undermining Spanish language development targets.

Core Arguments Presented
1
Translanguaging is not the absence of boundaries — it is the deliberate, research-grounded use of a student's full linguistic repertoire in service of a specific proficiency goal. Without a language objective, it is not translanguaging pedagogy.
2
Strategic translanguaging must be planned at the lesson level — embedded into the lesson architecture, not added as an accommodation. The Raíces micro-framework shows what this looks like in practice: each Crear task specifies when, why, and how cross-linguistic production is invited.
3
Translanguaging and biliteracy are complementary, not competing — honoring a student's full linguistic identity strengthens, rather than undermines, their development of academic Spanish. Evidence from the Biliteracy Bridge Library text selection process supports this claim.
Relevance to Multilingual Learning Programs Lead

This presentation is direct evidence of the thought leadership and community engagement dimensions of this role — presenting research to a practitioner audience, translating scholarly concepts into actionable classroom tools, and building organizational visibility in the multilingual education space. SAAABE represents the exact community-of-practice network a Multilingual Learning Programs Lead cultivates: bilingual educators, school leaders, and community advocates working at the intersection of language, culture, and equity. The presentation was also a proof point for the kind of partner-facing communication and materials this role requires: clear, evidence-grounded, and practitioner-accessible.

Teaching Across Borders
Practitioner Narrative · Journal of Multilingual Education Research · Vol. 15: Voices from the Field · In Preparation · Abstract Due June 30, 2026
Article Overview
"Teaching Across Borders: One Educator's Response to Anti-Immigrant Rhetoric"
Journal of Multilingual Education Research (JMER), Vol. 15 · "Voices from the Field" · Practitioner Narrative

This practitioner narrative documents a classroom response to rising anti-immigrant rhetoric in South Texas — centering the story of a Venezuelan-born student whose cultural identity was being erased in the name of assimilation. The piece resists the flattening of immigrant identities under generalized "Latino" frameworks and argues for pedagogical practices that honor the cultural specificity of each student's origin, language, and lived experience. It is grounded in three years of classroom practice and one year of doctoral scholarship.

Three Core Pedagogical Practices
1
Explicit Academic Language Instruction — teaching the linguistic forms that immigrant students need to access academic content and assert their identities in academic settings, without replacing home language or cultural practice.
2
Curriculum Redesign Centering Immigrant Narratives — replacing or supplementing materials that render immigrant students invisible with texts and tasks that position immigrant experience as intellectually rich, academically rigorous, and worthy of scholarly attention.
3
Strategically Planned Translanguaging Tied to ACTFL Proficiency Goals — not as permission to code-switch, but as a deliberate instructional move that honors students' full linguistic repertoire while building toward documented proficiency milestones.
Relevance to Multilingual Learning Programs Lead

This piece is evidence of the scholarly writing, evidence-generation, and community advocacy capacities this role requires. A Multilingual Learning Programs Lead writes grant narratives, research summaries, case studies, and thought leadership pieces — all of which require the ability to translate practitioner experience into compelling, evidence-grounded prose for multiple audiences. This article does exactly that: it speaks to the research community through JMER's peer-reviewed venue, while centering a real student's story as the organizing evidence. It also demonstrates the equity-forward stance and community-impact orientation that defines this organization's mission. The abstract-to-publication timeline maps directly to the grant development and reporting cycles this role manages.
